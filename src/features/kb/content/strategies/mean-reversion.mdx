When the market is in a ranging regime, trend-following strategies will bleed your account through a thousand tiny cuts. In a range, breakouts fail, and price repeatedly snaps back to the average (the mean). 

Mean-reversion trading is the art of selling resistance and buying support, anticipating that the boundaries will hold.

## The Anatomy of a Range

Ranges occur when buyers and sellers reach a temporary equilibrium. 
- **Resistance:** The ceiling where sellers consistently step in.
- **Support:** The floor where buyers consistently step in.
- **The Mean:** The middle of the range, often acting as a magnet for price.

<Callout type="info" title="The Rubber Band Effect">
Think of price in a range like a rubber band. The further it stretches from the mean toward the extremes (support or resistance), the stronger the force snapping it back to the center.
</Callout>

## Strategy Blueprint

1. **Regime Identification:** Confirm the market is ranging. You need at least two touches of a high and two touches of a low to define a valid range.
2. **The Setup:** Wait for price to approach the extreme boundaries. Do not trade in the middle of the range (the chop zone).
3. **The Trigger:** Look for exhaustion at the extremes. This often looks like a long wick piercing the boundary and immediately failing (a deviation or liquidity sweep).
4. **Execution:** Enter a short at resistance or a long at support *after* the exhaustion candle closes.
5. **Targets:** 
   - Target 1 (Aggressive): The mid-point (the mean) of the range.
   - Target 2 (Runner): The opposite end of the range.
6. **Stop Loss:** Place the stop just outside the extreme boundary. If the range breaks, you must exit immediately.
