# Why Gold Moves

Gold (XAUUSD) is a unique asset. Unlike equities, it yields no dividends. Unlike fiat currencies, it is not backed by a central bank or government. Its price is entirely driven by its status as a store of value, a safe-haven asset, and a hedge against systemic risk and inflation.

## The Triad of Gold Drivers

To understand why gold moves, traders must look at three primary macroeconomic drivers that directly influence institutional capital allocation:

1. **The US Dollar (DXY)**: Gold is priced in US Dollars. A stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for foreign buyers, reducing demand and lowering prices. A weaker dollar has the opposite effect.
2. **Real Interest Rates**: Real yields (nominal yield minus expected inflation) represent the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset like gold. When real yields rise, gold becomes less attractive compared to Treasury bonds.
3. **Geopolitical Risk & Market Sentiment**: During times of war, financial crises, or extreme uncertainty, capital flees riskier assets (equities) and seeks the safety of gold.

<ConceptCard title="The Opportunity Cost of Gold">
  Because holding physical gold or gold derivatives pays no interest, institutional investors weigh the cost of capital against potential price appreciation. If 10-Year Treasuries are yielding 5% while inflation is at 2% (Real Yield = 3%), holding gold is "expensive" compared to a risk-free 3% real return.
</ConceptCard>

## Inflation vs. Real Yields

A common misconception is that gold always rises when inflation rises. This is only true if inflation rises *faster* than interest rates. If a central bank hikes rates aggressively to combat inflation, real yields can actually rise, causing gold to sell off despite high headline inflation.
