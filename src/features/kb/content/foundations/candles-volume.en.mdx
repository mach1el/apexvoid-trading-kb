Every candlestick tells a story of a battle between buyers and sellers over a specific period of time. If you can read the candle, you can read the battle. 

{/* 2. Definition */}
**Candlesticks** are visual representations of price movement over a defined timeframe, displaying the Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC). **Volume** represents the total amount of the asset traded during that same period.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## Anatomy of a Candlestick

A candlestick is formed by four distinct data points during its lifecycle (e.g., during 1 hour for an H1 candle):
1. **Open:** The first price traded when the period begins.
2. **High:** The highest price traded during the period.
3. **Low:** The lowest price traded during the period.
4. **Close:** The last price traded when the period ends.

The area between the Open and the Close is called the **Body**. The body is shaded (or colored) to indicate the net direction of price.
- **Bullish Candle (Expansion):** The Close is *higher* than the Open. Buyers won the period. Usually colored green or blue.
- **Bearish Candle (Contraction):** The Close is *lower* than the Open. Sellers won the period. Usually colored red or orange.

The thin lines extending above and below the body are called **Wicks** (or shadows). They represent the extremes of price action during the period where price was pushed, but ultimately rejected before the close. 

## Reading the Story

The size of the body relative to the wicks tells you who is in control:
- **Large Body, Small Wicks:** Total dominance by one side. A massive bullish body indicates aggressive buying pressure (momentum).
- **Small Body, Large Wicks:** Indecision. A candle with a small body and huge wicks on both sides indicates a violent battle where neither buyers nor sellers could secure ground. 
- **Small Body, One Huge Wick (Pin Bar):** A strong rejection. If price pushes way down, but then violently reverses to close near the open, leaving a long lower wick, it indicates intense buying pressure stepping in to reject lower prices.

## The Role of Volume

Volume measures effort. Price measures result. If price pushes up strongly (a large bullish candle) on very high volume, the move is validated by massive participation. It is a genuine move.

If price pushes up strongly, but on extremely *low* volume, it is highly suspicious. It implies a lack of resistance rather than strong buying pressure. In institutional trading, anomalies between price spread (the size of the candle) and volume are the primary indicators of accumulation or distribution (a concept pioneered by Richard Wyckoff).

## How to Read Candle Context (Step-by-Step)

A single candlestick in isolation means nothing. A "bullish rejection" candle in the middle of nowhere is random noise. You must read candles in context.

1. **Locate Key Levels:** Only pay attention to candle shapes when price is interacting with a major HTF key level (support/resistance, PDH/PDL, Order Block).
2. **Observe the Approach:** Note the size of the candles approaching the level. Are they getting larger (accelerating momentum) or smaller (decelerating momentum)?
3. **Wait for the Close:** NEVER make a decision based on a forming candle. A candle that looks like a massive bearish breakout with 1 minute left can violently reverse and close as a bullish rejection pin bar. **The close is the only thing that matters.**
4. **Analyze the Reaction:** At the key level, does price print a high-volume rejection wick? Or does it print a large-body momentum candle blasting straight through it?

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Liquidity Sweep (Grounded)

**Scenario:** Gold (XAUUSD) is dropping towards yesterday's daily low (PDL).
1. As price hits the PDL, it accelerates downward, printing what looks like a massive bearish 15M candle breaking through support. Retail breakout traders rush in to sell.
2. In the final 3 minutes of the 15M period, massive buy volume steps in. 
3. The candle closes back above the PDL, leaving a long lower wick and a small bullish body. 

**Walkthrough:** 
This is a classic trap. The long lower wick proves that the move below the PDL was rejected. It wasn't a true breakout; it was a raid on liquidity (stop-losses of early buyers). The combination of the location (PDL) and the candle shape (long lower wick rejection) provides a high-probability bullish bias.

### Example 2: The Momentum Breakout (Idealized)

**Scenario:** AAPL is consolidating below $150 resistance.
1. Price pushes into $150 and prints three small Doji (indecision) candles right below the level. Volume is extremely low.
2. Suddenly, a massive 5M candle prints, closing at $152 with zero upper wick. Volume is 3x higher than average.

**Walkthrough:**
The tight consolidation (Dojis) on low volume showed a lack of selling pressure at resistance. The massive body breakout candle on high volume confirmed institutional sponsorship. This is not a fakeout; the sheer size of the body and the volume validate the move.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC/ICT:** The concepts of [Displacement](/price-action/displacement-imbalance) and [Fair Value Gaps (FVG)](/smc/fvg) rely entirely on large-body momentum candles. A rejection wick is often referred to as a [Liquidity Sweep](/smc/liquidity-sweep) or a "Judas Swing."
- **Wyckoff:** Volume Spread Analysis (VSA) is the entire basis of Wyckoff. High volume on small candle spread implies supply entering the market (Effort vs. Result).
- **Price Action:** Classic terminology uses "Pin Bars" for rejection, "Engulfing bars" for momentum shifts, and "Inside bars" for consolidation.

{/* 9. FAQ */}
## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need volume indicators to trade Forex?**
A: Forex is decentralized, so there is no central volume data. "Tick volume" provided by brokers is a proxy for activity, but it is not true traded volume. In Forex, price action (the size of the candles and speed of delivery) is your primary indicator of institutional involvement. In centralized markets like Stocks or Futures, real volume is crucial.

**Q: Why do some platforms color candles based on previous close?**
A: Traditional Japanese charting colors candles based on Open vs Close of the *current* candle. Some modern charting platforms (like Heikin Ashi) use complex averages, while others color based on whether the close is higher than the *previous* candle's close. Stick to standard OHLC coloring (Open vs Close of the current period) for pure price action analysis.

**Q: Are single candle patterns (like a Doji) enough to enter a trade?**
A: No. A single candle pattern only matters if it occurs at a highly significant structural level. A Doji in the middle of a range means nothing.
