Every technical indicator lags price. If you want to know what the market is doing *right now*, you must learn to read the raw footprint it leaves behind: market structure.

{/* 2. Definition */}
**Market structure** is the objective categorization of price trends based on the sequential relationship between peak highs and valley lows.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## Understanding the Mechanism of Structure

At its absolute core, financial markets do not move in straight lines. They breathe. They push in one direction (an impulsive move or expansion), and then they pull back to collect breath (a retracement or correction). This constant ebb and flow creates peaks and valleys on a chart, widely referred to as swing highs and swing lows.

Market structure is the study of how these swing points relate to one another. Charles Dow was one of the first to formalize this in the late 19th century [^1]. He observed that a rising market is characterized by a series of successive peaks that push higher than the last, accompanied by valleys that also form higher than the last. Conversely, a falling market forms a staircase stepping downward. 

This is not a subjective pattern; it is a mathematical necessity of an auction market. For an asset to increase in value over time, buyers must be willing to pay higher prices (breaking old highs), and during pullbacks, new buyers must step in at higher levels than before (forming higher lows). If this sequence is broken, the structural integrity of the trend is compromised.

There are three primary states of market structure:

1. **Uptrend (Bullish Structure):** A sequence of **Higher Highs (HH)** and **Higher Lows (HL)**. Buyers are in absolute control.
2. **Downtrend (Bearish Structure):** A sequence of **Lower Lows (LL)** and **Lower Highs (LH)**. Sellers are in absolute control.
3. **Consolidation (Ranging Structure):** Equal highs and equal lows, or highs and lows that fail to break out of a defined box. The market is in equilibrium, accumulating or distributing orders before the next trend.

Understanding this mechanism forces you to abandon predictions and focus on evidence. You do not need a moving average crossover to tell you the trend is up; if price is printing higher highs and higher lows, the trend is objectively up until it proves otherwise by breaking a previous higher low.

## How to Map Structure on a Chart (Step-by-Step)

Mapping structure correctly is the most important skill in technical analysis. Do not skip this process.

1. **Zoom Out:** Start on a higher timeframe (e.g., Daily or 4-Hour) to see the macro picture. You cannot map structure while zoomed into 1-minute noise.
2. **Identify the Extremes:** Find the absolute lowest point and highest point on the visible chart. These are your major swing points.
3. **Trace the Impulses:** Follow the price action from the lowest point up. When price pulls back significantly, mark the peak as a Swing High. 
4. **Mark the Retracements:** When price stops pulling back and begins pushing up again, breaking the previous peak, mark the valley as a Swing Low.
5. **Label the Sequence:** 
   - Is the new peak higher than the old peak? Label it **HH**.
   - Is the new valley higher than the old valley? Label it **HL**.
6. **Wait for Confirmation:** A Higher High is only confirmed once price pulls back. A Higher Low is only confirmed once price breaks the previous Higher High.

**Walkthrough:**
In this idealized scenario, price starts at a low, pushes up to form a peak, and pulls back. The moment price breaks above the first peak, the valley is confirmed as a Higher Low (HL), and the trend is officially bullish. Notice how every time price pulls back into the 'discount' of the previous leg, buyers step in to defend the structure. As long as the HLs remain intact, the trend remains bullish.

### Example 2: Grounded Bearish Breakdown

Imagine the EUR/USD 4-Hour chart. Price has been printing HHs and HLs for weeks. 
1. Price prints a new HH at 1.1000.
2. Price pulls back to 1.0900 (a new HL).
3. Price rallies but fails to break 1.1000, peaking at 1.0950. This is a Lower High (LH). 
4. Price aggressively sells off and breaks below the 1.0900 HL, reaching 1.0850. This is a Lower Low (LL).

**Walkthrough:** 
The structural integrity of the uptrend was compromised at step 3 when buyers failed to print a HH. However, the trend didn't officially reverse until step 4, when the previous HL was broken. This sequence (Failure to make HH → Break of HL) is the definitive signature of a structural shift from bullish to bearish.

### Counter-Example: The Internal Sweep (Trap)

A common mistake is misidentifying internal pullbacks as major structural breaks. If price is in a massive daily uptrend (from 1.0000 to 1.1000), a sharp 100-pip drop on a 15-minute chart will look like a sequence of LLs and LHs. However, this is merely internal structure (a retracement) within the macro leg. It is NOT a shift in the daily market structure until the daily HL is breached.

## Modern Framework Translations

Market structure is the oldest concept in trading, which means it has been renamed heavily by modern marketing.
- In **Smart Money Concepts (SMC)**, a break of structure in the direction of the trend is called a [Break of Structure (BOS)](/price-action/bos). A structural break that reverses the trend is called a [Change of Character (CHoCH)](/price-action/choch).
- In **ICT**, the exact same concepts are referred to as Market Structure Shifts (MSS). 
- In **Wyckoff**, the transition from bearish to bullish structure is detailed through accumulation schematics (Spring → Sign of Strength).

Regardless of what you call it, the mathematical reality is identical: Higher Highs and Higher Lows.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I map structure using candle bodies or wicks?**
A: This is highly debated, but the most robust approach is to map swings using the extreme wicks, because a wick represents a real price that was traded. However, for a structural *break* (BOS) to be confirmed, many professional traders require a candle *body* to close beyond the old wick to prove true displacement, rather than a mere liquidity sweep.

**Q: How do I know when a pullback is deep enough to be considered a new swing point?**
A: There is no universal fixed percentage. It depends on the asset's volatility. A good rule of thumb is that a valid swing point must visually retrace at least 30-50% of the previous impulse leg. Some traders use indicators like the Williams Fractal to mechanically define swings, but training your eyes is better long-term.

**Q: What if price makes a Higher High but then immediately makes a Lower Low?**
A: This is known as an expanding formation or a broadening wedge. It is a sign of high volatility and extreme indecision. In these conditions, structure is broken on both sides, rendering standard trend-following techniques dangerous. It's often best to stay out of the market until clear HH/HL or LL/LH structure resumes.
