{/* 1. Hook */}
Normally, financial markets move fluidly, with buyers and sellers exchanging assets at every single price point. But occasionally, a massive injection of institutional volume hits the market all at once. The price rockets (or plummets) so fast that the other side of the market is completely overwhelmed. This leaves behind a "gap" in the price action where no trading occurred. The market hates these gaps, and it will almost always return to fill them.

{/* 2. Definition */}
A **Fair Value Gap (FVG)**, also known as an Imbalance or Inefficiency, is a three-candle price pattern that reveals a vacuum of liquidity. 
- **Bullish FVG:** Occurs during a massive up-move. It is the gap between the High of Candle 1 and the Low of Candle 3.
- **Bearish FVG:** Occurs during a massive down-move. It is the gap between the Low of Candle 1 and the High of Candle 3.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Anatomy of an Imbalance

In a perfectly balanced, efficient market, the wicks of adjacent candles will overlap slightly. This overlap proves that both buyers and sellers had a chance to transact at those price levels. 

An FVG occurs when the middle candle (Candle 2) is so exceptionally large and violent that Candle 1 and Candle 3 do not touch each other.

Imagine a Bullish FVG:
1. **Candle 1:** Closes. Its high wick reaches $100.
2. **Candle 2 (Displacement):** Massive institutional buying occurs. Price rockets from $100 straight up to $110. 
3. **Candle 3:** Opens at $110, dips down to $108, and then continues up.

Notice the gap between $100 (Candle 1's High) and $108 (Candle 3's Low). During that $8 range, price moved so fast that *only* buyers were participating. There was zero two-sided trading. This is an inefficient price delivery. 

### Why the Market Fills the Gap
The market algorithms are designed to offer fair pricing to both buyers and sellers. When an FVG is left behind, the algorithm views it as unfinished business. Eventually, price will be magnetically drawn back down into that $100-$108 zone to offer sellers a chance to participate. This return to the FVG is called a **Fill** or **Mitigation**.

## How to Trade Fair Value Gaps

FVGs are incredibly versatile. They can be used as entry triggers, as targets, or as confirmation for Order Blocks.

1. **Identify the 3-Candle Pattern:** Look for massive displacement candles (Candle 2).
2. **Draw the Box:** Draw a rectangle from the High of Candle 1 to the Low of Candle 3. Extend it to the right.
3. **Wait for the Retrace:** Do not chase the displacement. Wait for price to naturally drift back into the FVG box.
4. **The Entry:** You can place a limit order at the start of the FVG, or wait for price to dip into the FVG and print a lower-timeframe reversal pattern before entering.
5. **The Stop Loss:** Place your stop loss completely outside the opposite end of the FVG. If price closes through the entire FVG, the setup is invalidated.

**Consequent Encroachment (CE):** This is the exact 50% midpoint of the FVG. Many institutional algorithms use the CE as their precise entry point. If price drops below the 50% mark of a Bullish FVG and closes there, it is a sign of weakness.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The FVG Continuation (Idealized)

**Scenario:** The S&P 500 (SPY) is in a strong Daily uptrend.
1. A massive green Daily candle forms, leaving a $5 Bullish FVG between Monday's high and Wednesday's low.
2. Thursday and Friday are slow, grinding red days. Price drifts downward into the FVG.
3. On Monday, price touches the exact 50% midpoint of the FVG (Consequent Encroachment).
4. Instantly, buyers step in. Price rallies, closing the day as a strong green hammer candle.
5. The uptrend resumes, breaking new highs.

**Walkthrough:**
This is the standard trend-continuation setup. The FVG acted as a magnet, drawing price back in to rebalance the order book. Once efficiency was restored at the 50% mark, the macro algorithmic buying resumed.

### Example 2: The Inversion Trap (Grounded)

**Scenario:** Bitcoin (BTC) leaves a massive Bullish FVG on the 4-Hour chart at $60,000.
1. You place a buy limit order at $60,000, expecting a bounce.
2. Price drops into the FVG. It doesn't pause. 
3. A massive 4-Hour red candle prints, slicing completely through the FVG and closing at $59,000. Your stop loss is hit.
4. Two hours later, price rallies back up to $60,000. 
5. Instead of breaking through, it treats $60,000 as *resistance*, rejects violently, and collapses to $55,000.

**Walkthrough:**
Why did it fail? Because macro momentum shifted. When a Bullish FVG is completely sliced through and closed below, it becomes an **Inversion FVG**. It flips from being support to becoming resistance. The failure of the FVG was actually a massive bearish signal.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC / ICT:** ICT popularized the term Fair Value Gap (FVG) and Consequent Encroachment (CE). It is arguably the most widely used concept in the SMC toolkit today.
- **Classic TA:** Classic charting sometimes refers to these as "Hidden Gaps" or "Single Prints" (in Market Profile theory). However, classic TA focuses heavily on true weekend gaps (where the market opens at a different price than it closed), whereas FVGs occur *during* continuous trading.
- **Order Flow:** In Volume Profile trading, an FVG correlates perfectly with a "Low Volume Node" (LVN)—an area where very little volume was transacted, which price will often revisit to build volume.

{/* 9. FAQ */}
## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does price have to fill the entire FVG?**
A: No. A partial fill (even just clipping the edge of the FVG) is often enough to restore balance and resume the trend, especially in highly aggressive momentum markets.

**Q: Can an Order Block and an FVG overlap?**
A: Yes! This is the ultimate setup. When a massive displacement leaves an FVG *and* an unmitigated Order Block right behind it, the overlapping zone is an incredibly high-probability Point of Interest (POI).

**Q: What if price wicks completely through the FVG but closes inside it?**
A: If it's just a wick, the FVG is still valid. The algorithm simply reached slightly deeper for liquidity. As long as the body of the candle does not close outside the FVG, the zone holds.
