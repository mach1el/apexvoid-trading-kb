Every trader has experienced this nightmare: You identify a strong support level. You buy the bounce. You place your stop loss exactly where every textbook tells you to—just below the support line. Price drops, hits your stop loss by two pips, and then immediately rockets to your profit target without you. You didn't get unlucky; you were the victim of a calculated Liquidity Sweep.

{/* 2. Definition */}
A **Liquidity Sweep** (often called a Stop Hunt) occurs when price aggressively pokes through a major pool of resting liquidity (an old high or low), triggers the orders resting there, and then rapidly reverses back into the range. It is the physical manifestation of institutions harvesting liquidity to execute massive orders.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Mechanics of a Sweep

As established in [Buy-Side & Sell-Side Liquidity](/smc/buyside-sellside-liquidity), pools of orders rest above old highs and below old lows. 

When a central bank wants to initiate a massive long position, they must find willing sellers. They do this by driving price *below* a major support level. 
1. **Triggering Stops:** Long traders hiding their stops below support are triggered (sell orders).
2. **Inducing Breakouts:** Breakout traders see the support breaking and initiate new short positions (sell orders).
3. **The Trap:** The bank now has an immense pool of sell orders to absorb. They buy heavily against these sellers. Once the bank's long orders are filled, they stop suppressing the price. The selling pressure vanishes, and price violently snaps back above the support level.

The defining characteristic of a sweep is the **failure to close** beyond the level. The price will *wick* through the liquidity pool, but the body of the candle will close back inside the range.

### Sweep vs. Break of Structure (BOS)
It is critical to distinguish between a Liquidity Sweep and a genuine [Break of Structure](/price-action/bos). 
- A **BOS** signals continuation. It is validated when the candle *closes* decisively beyond the old high/low.
- A **Sweep** signals reversal. It is validated when the candle *wicks* beyond the old high/low but *closes* back inside the range.

## How to Trade a Liquidity Sweep

Trading a sweep requires patience and discipline, as you are trading against the immediate momentum.

1. **Identify the Pool:** Locate a major pool of resting liquidity (e.g., Previous Daily High, Equal Lows, Asian Session High). Mark it with a horizontal line.
2. **Wait for the Raid:** Wait for price to aggressively push through the level. Do not enter as it breaks. 
3. **Wait for the Close:** Observe the candle close on your timeframe of choice (e.g., 15-Minute or 1-Hour). If it closes *back inside* the range, leaving a wick through the level, the sweep is confirmed.
4. **Enter the Reversal:** You can enter at market when the candle closes back inside, or drop to a lower timeframe and wait for a [CHoCH](/price-action/choch) to confirm the reversal before entering.
5. **Target Internal Liquidity:** If price sweeps Sell-Side Liquidity at the bottom of a range, target the Buy-Side Liquidity resting at the top of the range.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Asian High Sweep (Idealized)

**Scenario:** USD/JPY consolidates tightly during the Asian session, leaving a clear high at 140.50 and a low at 140.00.
1. As the London session opens, price spikes aggressively to 140.60.
2. Breakout traders buy the momentum, thinking a massive uptrend is starting. Early short sellers are stopped out.
3. The 15-Minute candle closes at 140.45. It leaves a massive wick poking above the Asian High, but the body closes back inside the range.
4. An order flow trader recognizes the BSL sweep. They enter a short position at 140.45, with a stop loss above the wick at 140.65.
5. Price immediately collapses, dropping 80 pips to sweep the Asian Low at 140.00.

**Walkthrough:**
This is the classic "Judas Swing" (false breakout). London volume was used to sweep Asian liquidity to fuel the true move of the day. Waiting for the candle close is what separated the trapped retail trader from the profitable institutional trader.

### Example 2: The Double Bottom Trap (Grounded)

**Scenario:** TSLA forms a Double Bottom at $200. It bounces twice, confirming it as "strong support" for retail traders.
1. A week later, TSLA approaches $200 again.
2. It breaks through, dropping rapidly to $195. 
3. A trader attempts to trade the sweep. They buy at $195 before the candle closes, assuming it's a stop hunt.
4. The 1-Hour candle closes at $195. The 4-Hour candle closes at $192.
5. TSLA continues to bleed for the next week, dropping to $170.

**Walkthrough:**
Why did it fail? Because the trader front-ran the close. A sweep is *only* a sweep if price is rejected and closes back inside the range. If the candle closes heavily outside the level, it is a genuine Break of Structure. The trader bought a falling knife instead of waiting for confirmation.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC / ICT:** ICT refers to this concept extensively. A sweep of a major high/low followed by a reversal is the core of the "Turtle Soup" strategy. It is also the defining feature of a "Judas Swing" during killzones.
- **Wyckoff:** In Wyckoff accumulation, a sweep of the trading range low is called a "Spring." In distribution, a sweep of the trading range high is called an "Upthrust." Both serve the exact same liquidity-harvesting purpose.
- **Classic TA:** Classic charting refers to this as a "False Breakout" or a "Fakeout."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What timeframe should I use to look for sweeps?**
A: Sweeps happen on all timeframes because the market is fractal. However, sweeps of 4-Hour or Daily highs/lows provide the most reliable and explosive reversals. A sweep of a 1-Minute high is mostly noise.

**Q: How do I know if the wick is long enough to be a sweep?**
A: It is less about the absolute length of the wick, and more about the *reaction*. If price pokes through a level and is immediately and violently rejected back into the range, it is a sweep.

**Q: If price sweeps a high, does that mean the trend is over?**
A: Not necessarily. A sweep of a minor internal high might just be a pullback in a macro uptrend. However, if price sweeps a major Weekly or Monthly high and rejects violently, it can signal a macro trend reversal.
