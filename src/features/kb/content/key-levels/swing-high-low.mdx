If you ask ten retail traders to map the swing highs and swing lows on a single chart, you will likely get ten completely different answers. Without an objective rule for defining a swing point, your technical analysis is just guessing based on what "looks right."

{/* 2. Definition */}
A **Swing High** is a peak in price where the highest candle is flanked by two candles with lower highs. A **Swing Low** is a valley where the lowest candle is flanked by two candles with higher lows. This is known as the objective 3-candle rule.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Mechanics of a Swing Point

In the study of [Market Structure](/foundations/market-structure), we established that markets move in sequences of impulses and pullbacks. The exact points where these legs reverse are the swing points. However, the market is noisy. If you consider every single red candle in an uptrend to be a "Swing High," you will be completely lost in the micro-structure, constantly thinking the trend has reversed when it has merely paused.

To filter this noise, institutional technical analysts rely on mechanical definitions. The most robust and universally accepted definition is the 3-candle fractal pattern (sometimes expanded to a 5-candle pattern, popularized by Bill Williams).

### The 3-Candle Swing High
A valid Swing High requires exactly three candles:
1. **Candle 1 (The Setup):** An initial candle pushing higher.
2. **Candle 2 (The Peak):** A candle whose High is strictly *higher* than the High of Candle 1, AND whose High is strictly *higher* than the High of Candle 3.
3. **Candle 3 (The Confirmation):** A candle whose High is strictly *lower* than the High of Candle 2.

### The 3-Candle Swing Low
A valid Swing Low requires the inverse:
1. **Candle 1 (The Setup):** An initial candle pushing lower.
2. **Candle 2 (The Valley):** A candle whose Low is strictly *lower* than the Low of Candle 1, AND whose Low is strictly *lower* than the Low of Candle 3.
3. **Candle 3 (The Confirmation):** A candle whose Low is strictly *higher* than the Low of Candle 2.

By adhering strictly to this rule, you remove emotion from chart mapping. If a peak does not have a lower high on *both* its left and its right, it is not a swing point. It is simply internal consolidation.

### Major vs. Minor Swings
Even with the 3-candle rule, a 1-minute chart will print hundreds of valid swing points a day. To trade effectively, you must distinguish between:
- **Minor Swings:** These form the internal structure of a single price leg. They are useful for scalping but do not dictate the overall trend.
- **Major Swings:** These are the prominent turning points that define the macro [Higher High / Higher Low](/foundations/market-structure) sequences. They visually stick out and often interact with Higher Timeframe Key Levels.

## How to Map Swings on a Chart (Step-by-Step)

If you are struggling to map structure, follow this exact mechanical process:

1. **Select the Timeframe:** Choose your anchor timeframe (e.g., the 1-Hour chart) and do not change it while mapping.
2. **Identify the Highest Peak:** Look at the last 100 candles and find the absolute highest wick. Label this as your initial major Swing High.
3. **Apply the 3-Candle Rule:** Verify that the candle to the immediate left and right of this peak have lower highs. If they don't, it's a complex consolidation, wait for a cleaner peak.
4. **Trace the Reversal:** From that peak, follow the price downward until you find the absolute lowest wick that is flanked by two higher lows. Label this the major Swing Low.
5. **Connect the Dots:** Draw a line from the Swing High to the Swing Low. You have now defined one complete structural leg. 
6. **Repeat:** Wait for the next valid 3-candle peak to form.

## Worked Examples

Let's look at how the 3-candle rule saves you from bad trades in real market environments.

### Example 1: The Inside Bar (Idealized)

**Scenario:** An uptrend is pushing higher. 
1. Candle 1 prints a large bullish candle with a High at $100.
2. Candle 2 prints another bullish candle with a High at $105. 
3. Candle 3 prints an "inside bar"—it is a bearish candle, but its High is only $104 and its Low is $101 (completely contained within Candle 2's range).
4. Candle 4 pushes up and closes with a High at $110.

**Walkthrough:** 
A retail trader sees the red Candle 3 and assumes $105 was a Swing High, and the subsequent move to $110 was a Break of Structure (BOS). This is incorrect. Because Candle 4 immediately broke higher, the $105 peak was never confirmed by a valid pullback. The entire sequence from $100 to $110 is a single impulsive leg. There is no Swing High until a proper 3-candle formation prints *after* $110.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC/ICT:** The distinction between major and minor swings is the entire basis of trading the [Premium / Discount EQ](/smc/premium-discount-eq) matrix. You must identify the true major Swing High and Low to draw your Fibonacci retracement accurately. Drawing Fibs on internal structure leads to failed [OTE](/ict/ote) setups.
- **Wyckoff:** Wyckoff practitioners use volume to validate swing points. A true Swing High (a Buying Climax) will typically be accompanied by ultra-high volume, whereas internal swings will occur on average or low volume.
- **Bill Williams:** Popularized the "Fractal" indicator, which is a mechanical 5-candle rule (two lower highs on the left, two on the right). It is a slightly stricter version of the 3-candle rule taught here.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I use the wicks or the bodies to define the swing point?**
A: Always use the extreme wicks. The wick represents the absolute highest or lowest price the market accepted during that period. Using bodies for swing identification will cause you to draw inaccurate ranges. 

**Q: If two candles have the exact same High, is it a Swing High?**
A: This forms a "Double Top" or [Equal Highs](/smc/buyside-sellside-liquidity) scenario. For a 3-candle swing to be strictly valid, there must be a singular peak. However, pragmatically, if Candle 2 and Candle 3 share the exact same high, and Candle 4 is lower, you can treat the double-high as the swing point. Be aware that equal highs are prime targets for liquidity sweeps.

**Q: Why do my swing points keep getting run over immediately after forming?**
A: You are likely identifying minor, internal swings in a ranging market, rather than major structural swings. If you are getting chopped up, zoom out one timeframe (e.g., move from the 15M to the 1H chart). The major swings will become immediately obvious, and the internal noise will disappear.
