# Who Was Richard Wyckoff?

Richard D. Wyckoff (1873–1934) was an American stock market pioneer. Alongside Dow, Gann, and Elliott, Wyckoff is considered one of the founding fathers of modern technical analysis. Unlike many who focused purely on math or geometry, Wyckoff analyzed the market based on its true driver: the intentions and footprint of the "Composite Operator" (smart money or institutional institutions).

He developed his methodology by observing the activities of successful operators of his time, such as Jesse Livermore and J.P. Morgan.

## Wyckoff's Three Fundamental Laws

The Wyckoff Method is underpinned by three foundational laws that govern market behavior.

### 1. The Law of Supply and Demand

This is the core principle of all price movement.
*   When Demand > Supply, prices rise.
*   When Supply > Demand, prices fall.
*   When Supply = Demand, prices consolidate.
Wyckoff traders use price action and volume to assess the balance of supply and demand over time.

### 2. The Law of Cause and Effect

In order to have an effect (a trend), you must first have a cause (a consolidation). 
*   **Cause:** The period of accumulation or distribution where smart money is building a position.
*   **Effect:** The subsequent markup (uptrend) or markdown (downtrend).
The size of the "cause" (the length of the sideways range) is directly proportional to the size of the "effect" (the resulting trend). Point and Figure charts were traditionally used by Wyckoff to measure this cause and project targets.

### 3. The Law of Effort vs. Result

This law looks at the divergence between volume (effort) and price action (result) to identify potential reversals or exhaustion.
*   If there is massive volume (high effort) but price barely moves (small result), it indicates that the dominant trend is meeting significant hidden resistance/support. 
*   For example, huge volume on a bullish breakout attempt that results in a tiny candle body closing near its lows suggests that smart money is selling heavily into the buying pressure.

<Callout type="info">
The Wyckoff Method is not about rigid, fixed patterns. It is a logic-based framework for interpreting the motives behind price and volume.
</Callout>
