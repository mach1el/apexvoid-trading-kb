# Evaluating Zone Quality

Not all supply and demand zones are created equal. Taking every zone that forms on a chart will lead to a low win rate. Traders must grade the quality of a zone before taking a trade off it.

## Key Factors for Zone Strength

1.  **Strength of the Move (Imbalance):** How aggressively did price leave the zone? A massive, near-vertical move indicates extreme institutional urgency and leaves behind unfilled orders. Sluggish departures are lower probability.
2.  **Break of Structure (BOS / CHoCH):** Did the move originating from the zone break a significant structural level? A zone that causes a major structural break is significantly stronger than one that just creates a minor swing.
3.  **Freshness:** Is this the first time price is returning to the zone? The first test of a supply/demand zone is always the highest probability. Subsequent tests consume the resting liquidity, making the zone weaker over time.
4.  **Time at Base:** How long did price consolidate before the impulsive move? A shorter consolidation (1-3 candles) often indicates aggressive, impatient institutional execution, creating a strong zone. Long, messy consolidations are often less reliable.
5.  **Liquidity Engineering:** Is there obvious liquidity (like a double bottom or a trendline) sitting just *before* the zone? If so, price is highly likely to sweep that liquidity and tap into your zone.

<Callout type="tip">
Always align your zones with the higher timeframe trend. Taking a 5-minute supply zone trade during a daily bullish trend is a recipe for getting stopped out.
</Callout>
