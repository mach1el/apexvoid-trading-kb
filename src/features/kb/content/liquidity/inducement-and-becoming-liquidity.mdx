# Inducement & Becoming Liquidity

In the context of market structure, **inducement** is a deliberate trap set by the market. It is a minor structural level or an obvious "clean" support/resistance line designed specifically to induce (convince) retail traders to enter positions early and place their stop losses in a vulnerable location.

## How Inducement Works

Imagine the market is in an overall downtrend, and smart money wants to sell at a premium price (a major Order Block or FVG higher up). 

To do this, they need retail traders to buy, providing the BSL required for smart money to sell. 
1. The market creates a minor pullback and forms a very obvious "resistance" level just *below* the true target (the Order Block). 
2. Retail traders see this resistance and assume the pullback is over. They are **induced** to go short early. 
3. They place their stop losses (buy orders) just above this minor resistance. 
4. The market then surges upward, sweeping those stops. The flurry of buy orders allows smart money to short heavily at the true Order Block. 

The minor resistance level was pure inducement.

## The Golden Rule of Liquidity

The most important lesson in this entire knowledge base is this: **Do not place your stop loss where everyone else does.**

If you are buying at a support level that has been touched 4 times and looks perfectly clean, and you place your stop loss 5 pips below that line, you are doing exactly what the algorithm wants you to do. You are not trading the market; you are providing the liquidity for someone else to trade the market.

<Callout type="warn" title="Becoming Liquidity">
If you enter a trade and you cannot clearly identify the liquidity pool the market is targeting, **you are the liquidity pool.**
</Callout>

How do you avoid becoming liquidity?
1. **Wait for the sweep:** Let the obvious level get swept, wait for the reversal (CHoCH), and enter *after* the manipulation has occurred.
2. **Hide your stops:** Place your stop loss beyond a major structural point that has *already* swept a liquidity pool.
