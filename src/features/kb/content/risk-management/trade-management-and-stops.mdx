# Trade Management & Stops

Entering a trade is only 10% of the job. Once the position is live, your sole responsibility is to manage the risk. 

Active trade management dictates how much profit you capture and how much capital you protect. Poor management turns winning trades into losers and keeps losing trades bleeding.

<Callout type="info" title="The Golden Rule of Stops">
Your initial stop loss must be placed at a level where the thesis of the trade is definitively proven wrong. It should never be placed based on an arbitrary dollar amount or pip count.
</Callout>

## Stop Placement

A stop loss is your absolute line in the sand. 

- **Structural Stops:** Placed above/below the swing high/low that created the setup. If this level is breached, the structure is broken.
- **Invalidation Stops:** Placed behind a specific Point of Interest (like an FVG or Order Block). If price completely disrespects the POI, the trade idea is invalid.

Never widen a stop once a trade is live. If the trade is failing, let it hit the stop. Wiping out days of progress because you "just needed to give it more room" is the hallmark of an amateur.

## Moving to Breakeven (BE)

Moving your stop to breakeven is a double-edged sword. Moving it too early results in getting stopped out on a retracement before the real move happens. Moving it too late risks giving back a full 1R loss on a trade that was deeply in profit.

A standard framework for moving to BE:
1. Wait for price to clear a major opposing liquidity pool or internal structure point.
2. Only move the stop to BE once the price has established a new structural swing that defends your entry.

## Partial Scale-Outs

Scaling out (taking partial profits) secures capital and reduces stress, but lowers your overall mathematical expectancy (because you are reducing the size on your winning trades while taking full size on your losing trades).

If you struggle with holding trades to target, taking 20-30% off at the first major opposing level can provide the psychological cushion needed to hold the remainder.
