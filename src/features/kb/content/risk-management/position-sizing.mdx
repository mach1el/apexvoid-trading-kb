{/* 1. Hook */}
Most new traders look at a setup, decide they feel "confident" about it, and arbitrarily decide to trade 1.0 lots. They place their stop loss 50 pips away because that's where the structure is. If the trade loses, they just lost $500. They have completely surrendered control of their risk to the market. Professional traders never let the market dictate their risk amount. They decide exactly how much money they are willing to lose *first*, and then calculate the lot size necessary to make the math work.

{/* 2. Definition */}
**Position Sizing** is the mathematical process of determining how many units (or lots) of an asset you should buy or sell. 
The gold standard for institutional and prop-firm trading is the **Fixed Fractional Risk** model. In this model, you risk a fixed percentage of your total account equity (e.g., 1%) on every single trade, regardless of how wide or tight the stop loss is.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Mathematics of Sizing

To properly size a position, you need three variables:
1. **Account Balance:** Your total trading capital (e.g., $10,000).
2. **Risk Percentage:** The strict fraction of your account you are willing to lose if the trade hits your stop loss (e.g., 1% = $100).
3. **Price Risk:** The physical distance between your Entry Price and your Stop Loss Price. 

### The Formula
The formula to calculate your position size is:
`Position Size = Risk Amount / (Price Risk × Contract Value)`

If you are trading Forex (where 1 standard lot = $10 per pip), and you have a 20-pip stop loss, your Price Risk × Contract Value is $200 per lot. If you only want to risk $100, you must trade 0.5 lots.
If your next trade has a 40-pip stop loss, your Price Risk × Contract Value is $400 per lot. To keep your risk at exactly $100, you must trade 0.25 lots.

As your stop loss gets wider, your position size gets smaller. **Your dollar risk always remains exactly the same.**

## Execution Workflow

Never guess your lot size. When you spot a setup on your chart, follow this strict mechanical workflow:

1. **Find the Entry & Stop:** Use your technical analysis (e.g., an [Order Block](/order-block/what-is-ob) mitigation) to determine exactly where you will enter, and exactly where your stop loss must be placed to invalidate the setup.
2. **Determine Price Risk:** Calculate the distance between those two points.
3. **Input to Calculator:** Plug your Account Balance, Risk %, Entry, and Stop into your sizing calculator.
4. **Execute:** The calculator will output a specific lot size (e.g., 0.33 lots). Go to your broker/platform and enter exactly 0.33 lots.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Tight Stop (Scaling Up)

**Scenario:** You have a $100,000 funded account. Your strict rule is to risk 1% per trade ($1,000).
1. You spot a pristine [Optimal Trade Entry](/ict/ote) on EUR/USD on the 5-minute chart.
2. The setup allows for a highly precise, tight stop loss of only 10 pips.
3. You plug this into the formula: $1,000 / (10 pips × $10/pip) = **10.0 Lots**.
4. You execute 10.0 lots. If it hits your stop, you lose exactly $1,000.

**Walkthrough:**
Because your technical analysis provided a tight stop loss, the mathematics allowed you to leverage up to a massive 10.0 lot position while still strictly adhering to your 1% risk limit.

### Example 2: The Wide Stop (Scaling Down)

**Scenario:** You have the exact same $100,000 account, risking 1% ($1,000).
1. You spot a macro swing trade on the 4-Hour chart. 
2. Because it is a higher timeframe, the structural stop loss must be placed 100 pips away to be safe from intraday noise.
3. You plug this into the formula: $1,000 / (100 pips × $10/pip) = **1.0 Lots**.
4. You execute 1.0 lots. If it hits your stop, you lose exactly $1,000.

**Walkthrough:**
The market demanded a wider stop, so the math forced your position size to shrink from 10.0 lots down to 1.0 lot. The dollar risk ($1,000) is identical to Example 1. This is how you survive all market environments.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **Prop Firms (FTMO, Topstep):** Fixed fractional sizing is the absolute mandatory baseline for surviving prop firm evaluations. Because they impose strict daily drawdown limits (e.g., 5%), risking more than 0.5% or 1.0% per trade mathematically guarantees you will fail the challenge during a normal statistical losing streak.
- **Kelly Criterion:** A more advanced, dynamic sizing model from mathematics/gambling. While mathematically optimal for maximizing growth, the Kelly Criterion often suggests risking 10-20% per trade, which results in drawdowns that are psychologically devastating for human traders. Fixed fractional (1-2%) is the pragmatic industry standard.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why base the risk on a percentage instead of a flat dollar amount (e.g., always $100)?**
A: Compounding. If you risk a fixed $100 on a $10,000 account, you are risking 1%. But if your account grows to $20,000, that $100 is now only 0.5% risk. You are throttling your compounding power. Conversely, if your account drops to $5,000, that $100 is now 2% risk. A flat dollar amount forces you to risk *more* of your account as you lose, which accelerates ruin. A fixed percentage naturally scales up when you win and scales down when you lose.

**Q: Is 1% the rule for everything?**
A: 1% is the standard for personal capital. However, if you are trading a funded prop firm account with a 10% max drawdown, you are actually only trading a $10,000 buffer on a "$100k account." In prop firms, professional traders typically risk 0.25% to 0.50% per trade to survive the tight drawdown rules.

**Q: What if I don't use a stop loss?**
A: Then position sizing is impossible, because your Price Risk is technically infinite (the asset going to zero). Trading without a stop loss is not trading; it is gambling.
