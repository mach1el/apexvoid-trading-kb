# Trend Mechanics (HH, HL, LH, LL)

<ConceptCard title="The Definition of a Trend" icon="📈">
Dow Theory defines an uptrend as a successive series of Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). Conversely, a downtrend is a successive series of Lower Highs (LH) and Lower Lows (LL). 
</ConceptCard>

Before Dow, traders looked at charts as chaotic, random wiggles. Dow was the first to formalize that prices move in zigzag patterns, and that by identifying the peaks and troughs of these zigzags, one could objectively define the direction of the market.

This exact definition is still the foundational core of every modern trading strategy, from basic retail Price Action to advanced Smart Money Concepts.

## The Anatomy of an Uptrend

In a healthy bull market, demand outstrips supply. 
- Buyers are aggressive, pushing the price to new extremes (Higher Highs).
- When buyers take profit, the price pulls back. However, because demand is so strong, eager buyers step in *before* the price can reach the previous low, creating a Higher Low.

**The Golden Rule:** An uptrend remains valid *until* price breaks below the most recent Higher Low. A single lower high is not enough to confirm a trend reversal; you must see a structural lower low.

## The Anatomy of a Downtrend

In a bear market, supply overwhelms demand.
- Sellers are aggressive, dumping the asset and pushing price to new depths (Lower Lows).
- When short-sellers take profit (buying to cover), the price briefly rallies. However, eager sellers short the asset again *before* it can reach the previous high, creating a Lower High.

**The Golden Rule:** A downtrend remains valid *until* price breaks above the most recent Lower High. 

<Callout type="warn" title="The Trap of the Early Reversal">
Retail traders constantly lose money trying to buy the exact bottom of a downtrend. Until the sequence of Lower Highs is broken, you are fighting the dominant flow of capital. Wait for structural confirmation.
</Callout>

## The Transition Phase (Consolidation)

Trends do not usually reverse instantly. There is almost always a transitional period where the sequence of HH/HL is broken, but a new sequence of LH/LL has not yet been established. 

This is known as a **Range** or **Consolidation**. 
In a range, the market prints Equal Highs and Equal Lows. According to Dow Theory, you should not deploy trend-following strategies during this phase. You must wait for the market to breakout and establish a new directional sequence.

## Application in Modern Frameworks

Dow's simple definition of trends is the direct ancestor of modern "Market Structure" concepts.

- When an uptrend prints a new Higher High, modern traders call this a **Break of Structure (BOS)**.
- When an uptrend fails to print a Higher High, and instead prints a Lower Low, modern traders call this a **Change of Character (CHoCH)**.

The terminology has evolved to sound more sophisticated, but the underlying mechanic is exactly the same as what Charles Dow observed in 1899.

<KeyTakeaways>
- An uptrend is a series of Higher Highs and Higher Lows.
- A downtrend is a series of Lower Highs and Lower Lows.
- A trend remains in effect until the structural sequence is broken.
- Modern terms like "BOS" and "CHoCH" are just rebranding of Dow's original trend definitions.
</KeyTakeaways>
