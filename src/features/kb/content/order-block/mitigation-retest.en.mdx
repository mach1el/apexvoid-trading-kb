Spotting a massive institutional Order Block is useless if you don't know how to trade it. The trade does not happen when the block is formed; the trade happens hours, days, or even weeks later when price inevitably returns to the scene of the crime. This return is the Mitigation.

{/* 2. Definition */}
**Mitigation (or Retest)** is the process where price returns to a previously untested Order Block. The purpose of this return is for massive institutions to close out the "drawdown" positions they initiated to manipulate the market prior to the original displacement.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Mechanics of Mitigation

As established in [What is an Order Block](/order-block/what-is-ob), banks often have to sell into the market to engineer enough liquidity to execute their massive buy orders. 

When the price rockets upward (the displacement), those initial sell orders are left heavily in the negative (drawdown). A central bank does not simply take a massive loss on those shorts. Their algorithms are designed to slowly draw price back down to that exact point of origin. 

When price touches the Bullish Order Block, the bank closes their short positions at breakeven. 
To close a short position, you must *buy* the asset. 

This massive influx of buying pressure (from the bank covering their shorts, plus new long orders being added) is what causes price to violently bounce off the Order Block. Once the orders are closed, the block is considered **Mitigated**, and the institutional objective is complete. The true macro trend can now resume.

### The First Touch Rule
Because mitigation is the act of consuming resting orders, an Order Block is a finite resource. 
- **The First Touch (Unmitigated):** High probability. The institutional orders are resting there, waiting to be triggered.
- **The Second Touch (Mitigated):** Low probability. Most of the orders were consumed on the first touch. The level is now weak.
- **The Third Touch:** Highly likely to fail. Price will likely slice right through it to seek liquidity lower down.

## How to Execute the Retest (Step-by-Step)

There are two primary ways to enter a trade during the mitigation phase:

### Method 1: The Limit Entry (Aggressive)
1. Identify an unmitigated HTF Order Block.
2. Place a Buy Limit order precisely at the top edge (the proximal line) of the OB box.
3. Place your Stop Loss slightly below the bottom edge (the distal line) of the OB box.
4. **Pros:** You will never miss the trade.
5. **Cons:** If you are wrong about the context, you will take a full loss as price slices through.

### Method 2: The Confirmation Entry (Conservative)
1. Identify an unmitigated HTF Order Block.
2. Set a price alert at the top edge of the box. Do not place a limit order.
3. When the alert triggers, drop to a Lower Timeframe (e.g., 5-Minute).
4. Watch price tap into the HTF OB. Wait for the 5-Minute chart to print a [Change of Character (CHoCH)](/price-action/choch) to the upside.
5. Enter on the pullback of that 5-Minute CHoCH.
6. **Pros:** Extremely high win rate, as you wait for structural proof that the banks are defending the level.
7. **Cons:** You might miss the trade if price taps the OB and rockets away instantly without offering a LTF pullback.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Perfect Limit Fill (Idealized)

**Scenario:** USD/JPY prints a 1-Hour Bullish Order Block between 145.00 and 144.80.
1. You place a Buy Limit at 145.00.
2. You place your Stop Loss at 144.75 (5 pips below the block).
3. Over the next three hours, price slowly grinds down.
4. At 8:30 AM (New York session open), price drops rapidly, perfectly tapping 145.00.
5. Your limit order is triggered.
6. Instantly, massive volume enters the market. The 15-Minute candle closes as a huge bullish hammer. Price never looks back, rallying 100 pips to 146.00.

**Walkthrough:**
This is the textbook mitigation. The bank returned to 145.00 to close their drawdown shorts and immediately stepped on the gas. Your limit entry caught the exact bottom of the move with zero drawdown.

### Example 2: The Mitigation Failure (Grounded)

**Scenario:** Gold prints a 4-Hour Bullish OB. 
1. Price returns to the zone two days later. 
2. You use the Conservative Method. You drop to the 15-Minute chart and wait for a CHoCH.
3. Price taps the 4-Hour OB, but the 15-Minute chart continues to print Lower Highs and Lower Lows. 
4. It never prints a CHoCH. Instead, it slices straight through the bottom of the 4-Hour OB.
5. You never entered the trade.

**Walkthrough:**
This is why the Confirmation Entry is so powerful. Why did the OB fail? Perhaps macro news changed the banking bias, or perhaps this OB was just an inducement trap (which we will cover next in [Real vs Fake OBs](/order-block/real-vs-fake)). Because you waited for LTF confirmation that never came, you saved yourself from a losing trade.

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC / ICT:** ICT explicitly teaches the concept of the "Return to Origin" (RTO) or mitigation block. The fundamental rule that "Order Blocks are one-time use" is a core SMC tenet.
- **Wyckoff:** The mitigation phase perfectly mirrors the "Test" phase of a Wyckoff schematic, where price dips back into the accumulation range to ensure supply has been exhausted before the markup phase begins.
- **Classic TA:** Classic charting calls this a "Throwback" (in an uptrend) or a "Pullback" (in a downtrend) to a broken resistance-turned-support. However, classic TA often advocates buying the 3rd or 4th touch, which contradicts institutional order flow logic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long can an Order Block remain unmitigated?**
A: Days, weeks, or even years. On a Monthly chart, an Order Block left behind in 2018 might not be mitigated until 2024. The institutional orders (or algorithmic memory) remain there until price finally returns.

**Q: What if price misses my limit order by 1 pip and runs away?**
A: This happens. Institutional algorithms aren't perfect, and sometimes they front-run their own levels if liquidity is thin. This is the cost of doing business. Do not chase the trade if it misses your entry and rockets away. 

**Q: Should I trade mitigations during major news events (like NFP or CPI)?**
A: No. Major news events are often used by algorithms as an excuse to completely smash through obvious Order Blocks to grab deeper liquidity. It is highly erratic and unpredictable.
