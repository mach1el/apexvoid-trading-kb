{/* 1. Hook */}
Retail traders buy and sell at market price, executing instantly. A central bank or a massive hedge fund cannot do this. If they need to accumulate a billion-dollar position, they must do so strategically, hiding their intent, and building their position over time. The "Order Block" is the last footprint they leave before they are fully positioned and ready to send the price flying.

{/* 2. Definition */}
An **Order Block (OB)** is typically defined as the last opposing candle (or series of candles) prior to a strong impulsive move (Displacement) that results in a Break of Structure. 
- A **Bullish OB (Demand)** is the last down candle before a massive up move.
- A **Bearish OB (Supply)** is the last up candle before a massive down move.

{/* 3. Deep Explanation */}
## The Institutional Logic

To understand *why* an Order Block works, you must understand institutional order flow. 

Imagine a massive bank wants to buy EUR/USD. If they buy all at once, they will cause extreme slippage, ruining their average entry price. Instead, they engineer liquidity. 

First, they heavily *sell* the market. This pushes price down, triggering retail stop-losses and inducing breakout traders to go short. By forcing everyone else to sell, the bank has created a massive pool of willing sellers. The bank then uses all those retail sell orders to fill their own massive *buy* orders without slipping the price. 

Once their buy orders are filled, they stop selling. The immense buying pressure takes over, resulting in a massive, violent move upward (Displacement).

### The Footprint Left Behind
That initial move down—the selling they had to do to accumulate their longs—is the **Bullish Order Block**. 

Because they had to sell to manipulate the price, they are technically in "drawdown" on those initial sell positions as the price rockets upward. As highly risk-averse entities, they will eventually maneuver price back down to that exact zone to close their sell positions at breakeven. When they close those shorts (buying to cover), it adds more buying pressure to the market, causing price to bounce perfectly off the Order Block.

## The 3 Pillars of a Valid Order Block

A random down candle before an up candle is NOT an Order Block. To be valid, it must meet three strict criteria:

1. **It Must Displace:** The move originating from the OB must be violent. It should leave behind a [Fair Value Gap (FVG)](/price-action/displacement-imbalance). If price slowly grinds away from the candle, it is not an institutional OB.
2. **It Must Break Structure:** The displacement must achieve something. It must result in a valid [BOS](/price-action/bos) or [CHoCH](/price-action/choch). An OB that fails to break structure is weak.
3. **It Must Be Unmitigated:** Price must not have returned to the OB yet. Once price taps into the OB and bounces, the institutional orders are filled, and the OB is considered "mitigated" (used up).

{/* 6. Worked Examples */}
## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Pristine Demand Block (Idealized)

**Scenario:** Apple (AAPL) is in an uptrend on the Daily chart.
1. Price pulls back for three days, creating a series of red candles.
2. On Thursday, a massive green candle prints, breaking above the previous Swing High (BOS). 
3. This green candle is huge and leaves a Fair Value Gap behind it.
4. The last red candle on Wednesday is our Bullish Order Block. 
5. We draw a box from Wednesday's high to Wednesday's low and extend it to the right. 
6. Two weeks later, price drops into that box, taps the top of it, and immediately rockets to new all-time highs.

**Walkthrough:**
This is the textbook setup. The displacement proved institutional intent. The BOS validated the level. And because it had never been tested, we knew it was unmitigated. The return to the OB offered a low-risk, high-reward entry.

### Example 2: The Failed "Block" (Grounded)

**Scenario:** EUR/USD is chopping in a tight, 20-pip range on the 15-Minute chart.
1. Price drops slightly, printing a red candle.
2. The next candle is green, but it is small. It barely pushes past the previous red candle.
3. It does not break any major swing highs. It leaves no FVG.
4. A retail trader marks that red candle as an Order Block and places a buy limit order on it.
5. An hour later, price easily slices straight through the box, stopping the trader out.

**Walkthrough:**
Why did it fail? Because it was missing the pillars. There was no displacement, and there was no structural break. A down candle in a choppy, sideways market is just noise. An Order Block requires proof of institutional sponsorship (violent speed and structural shifts).

## Modern Framework Translations

- **SMC / ICT:** The term "Order Block" is the cornerstone of ICT methodology. ICT specifically defines a bullish OB as the lowest candle with a down close that has the most range between open and close, near a support level.
- **Supply & Demand Trading (Sam Seiden):** S&D traders refer to Order Blocks as "Drop-Base-Rally" (Demand) or "Rally-Base-Drop" (Supply) zones. The underlying logic of institutional imbalance is exactly the same.
- **Wyckoff:** In Wyckoff terminology, a bullish Order Block often coincides with the "Spring" or the "Test" phase in an Accumulation schematic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I draw the OB box using just the candle body, or the wicks too?**
A: Institutional purists draw the box encompassing the entire candle (high to low, including wicks). However, if the wick is unusually long (making the risk too wide), many traders refine the zone by drawing it only on the candle body (open to close), or dropping to a lower timeframe. We will cover this in [Refining OBs](/order-block/identify-manually).

**Q: What if there are three consecutive red candles before the massive green displacement? Which one is the OB?**
A: All three! When you have a series of consecutive down-close candles prior to displacement, the entire sequence is considered one macro Order Block. You draw your zone from the highest high of the series to the lowest low of the series.

**Q: Can I trade Order Blocks against the trend?**
A: You can, but it is much lower probability. A bullish Order Block formed in a macro downtrend is often just a temporary pause before price runs through it. Always trade OBs that align with the Higher Timeframe trend.
