# Price Action & Market Structure

Price action is the purest, most unfiltered form of market data available. Before indicators print a signal, before moving averages cross, the price has already moved. For a scalper operating on the 1-minute (M1) and 5-minute (M5) timeframes, mastering price action and market structure is not optional—it is the absolute foundation of your edge. 

By reading the narrative behind every candlestick and meticulously mapping out the micro-structural shifts, a scalper can determine precisely who is in control of the market, where the liquidity lies, and when a reversal is statistically probable.

## The Micro-Battle Within the Candlestick

Every single candlestick on a chart represents a violent, real-time struggle between buyers (bulls) and sellers (bears) over a specific time period. On an M1 chart, this battle occurs in 60-second intervals. The anatomy of a candle—its body, its upper wick, its lower wick, and where it opens and closes—tells a detailed story of rejection, continuation, exhaustion, or indecision.

Understanding candlestick anatomy goes beyond memorizing names like "Doji" or "Hammer." It requires you to visualize the order flow that created the candle.

- **The Body:** The distance between the open and close. A large, full body indicates strong conviction and momentum from one side. If an M5 candle opens at 1.1000 and closes at 1.1020 with almost no wicks, it means buyers were in absolute, uncontested control for those 5 minutes.
- **The Wicks:** The wicks represent the extremes of the battle. A long wick indicates a price level that was tested but ultimately rejected. This is a footprint of trapped traders.

  - **The Pin Bar (Hammer / Shooting Star):** A candle with a small body and an exceptionally long wick on one side.   - **The Indecision Doji:** A candle where the open and close are virtually identical, with wicks on both sides. On an M1 chart, this often precedes an explosive breakout, as both sides have exhausted themselves fighting to a draw, and the market is waiting for a new influx of volume.

## Sóng (Market Swings): Impulse vs. Correction

The market never moves in a perfectly straight line, not even on the micro-timeframes. It breathes. It moves in waves or swings (Sóng). Understanding the alternating nature of these swings is critical for timing your entries with millimeter precision.

### 1. Impulse Swings
Impulse swings are the strong, aggressive, violent moves in the direction of the underlying trend. They are characterized by large momentum candles, very little overlapping price action, and a rapid change in price. This is when institutional volume is actively driving the market.
*Example:* Price explodes from $100.00 to $105.00 in just three consecutive 5-minute bullish candles. This is a massive display of buyer dominance.

### 2. Correction (Pullback) Swings
Correction swings are the slow, sluggish, weak moves against the primary trend. They represent profit-taking from the impulse move and a lack of conviction from the counter-trend participants. Corrections typically feature smaller, choppy candles, wicks in both directions, and heavy overlapping price action as the market grinds slowly back to a value area.
*Example:* Following the impulse to $105.00, the price takes 12 separate 5-minute candles to slowly bleed back down to $102.50. The fact that it took 15 minutes to go up $5, but 60 minutes to go down $2.50, tells you that the bears have no real power. The underlying trend remains bullish.

Scalpers do not trade the middle of an impulse; they wait for the sluggish correction to exhaust itself at a logical support level, and they enter just as the next violent impulse swing begins.

## Mapping Market Structure

Market structure is the framework that gives context to candlestick patterns. It is defined by the ongoing sequence of swing highs and swing lows. If you cannot correctly map market structure, you are trading blind.

- **Uptrend (Bullish Structure):** The market is making a staircase pattern upwards, characterized by Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). The rule of an uptrend is simple: As long as the most recent Higher Low (HL) remains unbroken, the buyers are completely in control. You should only be looking for long setups at pullback areas.
- **Downtrend (Bearish Structure):** The market is stair-stepping downwards, characterized by Lower Lows (LL) and Lower Highs (LH). As long as the most recent Lower High (LH) is defended, sellers dominate the tape. You should only be looking to short rallies.

## True Structure Breaks (BOS) vs. Liquidity Sweeps

This is perhaps the most crucial distinction a scalper must master. On the M1 and M5 timeframes, the market will constantly attempt to trick you into believing the structure has shifted.

A **Break of Structure (BOS)** occurs when the market genuinely shifts its trend. For example, in an uptrend, the price drops and completely violates the last Higher Low (HL). However, to be a true BOS, you must see a strong, decisive candle *body close* below the level. This indicates that participants have accepted the new lower pricing.

A **Liquidity Sweep (Fake-out)** occurs when the price temporarily drops below the last Higher Low (HL) merely to trigger stop losses, but it immediately rejects and closes back above the level, leaving behind a long wick. 

*Step-by-Step Scenario:*
1. The market is in an M5 uptrend. The last Higher Low is at 4500.00.
2. Price pulls back aggressively to 4498.00. Retail traders panic, assume the trend is over, and sell. Breakout traders see support broken and initiate shorts.
3. Within the exact same 5-minute candle, institutional buyers absorb all that selling, and the candle closes back up at 4502.00.
4. The result? A long lower wick piercing 4500.00. The structure did not break; it was a liquidity sweep. The uptrend is fully intact, and this sweep provides the perfect, high-probability entry for a scalper to go long.
