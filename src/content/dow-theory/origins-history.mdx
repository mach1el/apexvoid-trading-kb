# Origins & History

<ConceptCard title="A Posthumous Theory" icon="📜">
Charles H. Dow never published a book called "Dow Theory" nor intended to create a strict trading system. His ideas were distilled posthumously from his editorials in *The Wall Street Journal* (1899–1902) by his successors, William Peter Hamilton and Robert Rhea.
</ConceptCard>

To truly understand Dow Theory, one must separate Charles Dow's original market observations from the formal systems codified by later analysts. Much of what modern traders attribute to Dow was actually formalized decades after his death.

---

## 1. Charles H. Dow (1899–1902)

Charles Dow was a journalist, co-founder of Dow Jones & Company, and the first editor of *The Wall Street Journal*. His primary interest was macroeconomic forecasting, not day trading. 

Dow created two vital indices to serve as barometers for the broader U.S. economy:
1. **The Industrial Average:** Representing the manufacturing sector.
2. **The Rail Average:** (Now the Transportation Average) Representing the distribution of those manufactured goods.

Between 1899 and his death in 1902, Dow wrote approximately 250 editorials observing that market prices were not random. He proposed that the stock market as a whole was a reliable leading indicator of business conditions. He likened market movements to the ocean—having tides, waves, and ripples.

---

## 2. William Peter Hamilton (1922)

Following Dow's death, his successor at *The Wall Street Journal*, William Peter Hamilton, spent over 20 years applying and testing Dow’s concepts in real-time. 

In 1922, Hamilton published ***The Stock Market Barometer***. This was the first attempt to synthesize Dow’s editorial insights into a cohesive framework. Hamilton demonstrated how market averages could forecast business conditions, explicitly referring to the market as a "barometer" that discounted all future events.

---

## 3. Robert Rhea (1932)

If Hamilton proved the concepts worked, Robert Rhea transformed them into a rigorous, systematic methodology.

In 1932, while confined to his bed by illness, Rhea published ***The Dow Theory***. He meticulously studied all 252 editorials by Dow and Hamilton, codifying their fragmented ideas into clear, actionable rules for investors. 

<Callout type="note" title="The Architect of the Tenets">
The famous "Six Tenets" of Dow Theory—the structural rules taught to every technical analyst today—were formulated and categorized by Robert Rhea, not Charles Dow himself. Rhea shifted the focus slightly from macroeconomic forecasting toward practical stock market speculation.
</Callout>

---

## 4. Richard Russell & Modern Continuations

The lineage of Dow Theory continued through practitioners like Richard Russell, who wrote the *Dow Theory Letters* for decades, adapting the theory to post-war modern markets. Today, the theory forms the DNA of almost every trend-following system in existence.

<KeyTakeaways>
- Charles Dow did not invent a formal trading system; he wrote observational editorials about market trends.
- William Peter Hamilton (1922) proved the predictive power of Dow's market averages in *The Stock Market Barometer*.
- Robert Rhea (1932) formally codified the "Six Tenets" in his book *The Dow Theory*.
- Distinguishing between Dow's original intent (macro forecasting) and Rhea's codification (speculative methodology) is crucial for historical accuracy.
</KeyTakeaways>
